Chapter 9.2: Identity Theft Prevention and Recovery - Learning Lab

Developing practical strategies to protect your identity and recover from identity theft

Learning Objectives

By the end of this Learning Lab, you will be able to:

  • Evaluate various tactics and scenarios used by identity thieves
  • Apply practical prevention strategies to protect your personal information
  • Design a personalized identity theft protection plan
  • Analyze real-world identity theft cases and recommend recovery steps
  • Assess personal vulnerability to identity theft and create mitigation strategies

Introduction

In today's digital world, identity theft has become increasingly common. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) reported that identity theft was the most common type of fraud reported in recent years, affecting millions of Americans. This Learning Lab will help you develop practical skills to protect yourself from identity theft and respond effectively if it happens to you.

Important Note

The activities in today's Learning Lab will help you create a comprehensive identity protection plan. Each station builds toward the final protection plan, so be sure to save your work along the way. The skills you develop today could save you from financial loss and stress in the future.

Connection to Day 1

In Day 1, we explored the fundamental concepts of identity theft, including:

  • Common tactics used by identity thieves (digital and physical methods)
  • Impact of identity theft on victims' finances and credit
  • Warning signs that your identity may have been stolen
  • Basic prevention strategies and recovery steps

Today, we'll build on this knowledge by applying these concepts through practical exercises and developing your own comprehensive identity protection plan.

Identity Theft Simulation

Estimated time: 15 minutes

In this simulation, you'll analyze different scenarios where personal information might be at risk. For each scenario, identify how the information could be compromised and recommend protection strategies.

Identity Theft Risk Scenarios

Scenario 1: Social Media Exposure

Jordan regularly posts updates about their life on social media, including:

  • Birthday celebration photos with date
  • Vacation plans with specific dates they'll be away
  • "Throwback" photos from childhood showing their high school name
  • Photos of a new car purchase with visible license plate
  • Pet photos with the pet's name in captions

Scenario 2: Public Wi-Fi Usage

Taylor frequently works from coffee shops using public Wi-Fi and:

  • Checks bank account balances
  • Makes online purchases with credit card
  • Logs into email and social media accounts
  • Completes school financial aid forms
  • Uses the same password for multiple accounts

2

Case Study Analysis

Estimated time: 15 minutes

In this station, you'll analyze real-world identity theft cases and develop response plans. These case studies will help you understand both the prevention and recovery aspects of identity theft.

Case Study: Tax Identity Theft

Victim: Alex, a 22-year-old college student working part-time

Situation: When Alex filed their tax return in February, it was rejected by the IRS with a message stating that a return had already been filed using their Social Security Number. Someone had filed a fraudulent return using Alex's identity and claimed a large refund. Alex was not aware of any data breaches or suspicious activity on their accounts prior to this incident.

Impact: Alex's legitimate tax refund of $1,200 was delayed by 6 months. They had to file additional paperwork with the IRS, place fraud alerts on their credit reports, and spend hours resolving the issue.

Analysis Questions

Case Study: Credit Card Fraud

Victim: Morgan, a 19-year-old who recently got their first credit card

Situation: Morgan received a notification from their credit card company about suspicious activity. Upon checking their account, they discovered $1,500 in charges at online electronics retailers that they didn't make. The purchases were made within the last 48 hours, and Morgan still had their physical card in their possession.

Additional context: Morgan recently made several online purchases on their laptop while connected to public Wi-Fi at a local coffee shop. They also took a photo of their new credit card and sent it to a friend through a messaging app to show off their first credit card.
