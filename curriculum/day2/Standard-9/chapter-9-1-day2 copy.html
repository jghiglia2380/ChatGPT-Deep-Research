Chapter 9.1: Protecting Yourself from Consumer Fraud

Learning Lab: Applying consumer fraud protection strategies through interactive activities

Learning Objectives

  • Identify different types of consumer fraud including phishing, spoofing, and identity theft
  • Recognize warning signs of potential scams and fraudulent activities
  • Apply strategies to protect personal and financial information
  • Understand consumer rights and resources for reporting suspected fraud
  • Develop skills to educate others about avoiding consumer fraud

Introduction

Welcome to the Consumer Fraud Protection Learning Lab! Today, we'll build on what we learned about different types of consumer fraud and develop practical skills to protect ourselves and others from scams. Through hands-on activities, you'll analyze real examples of consumer fraud, create a personalized protection plan, and develop educational materials to help others stay safe.

Important Note

Consumer fraud is constantly evolving with new scams appearing regularly. The skills you develop today will help you recognize warning signs across different types of fraud, even as scammers adapt their tactics. Remember: if something seems too good to be true, it probably is!

Connection to Day 1

In Day 1, we learned about the different types of consumer fraud including phishing, spoofing, identity theft, and other scams. We explored the warning signs of fraud and introduced basic protection strategies. Today's activities will put that knowledge into action by analyzing real scams, developing personalized protection plans, and creating resources to educate others about fraud prevention.

Scam #1: Email from "Your Bank"

URGENT: Your Online Banking Access Suspended

From: SecureBankingAlert@secure-banking-center.com

Dear Valued Customer,

Our security system has detected unusual activity on your account. Your access has been temporarily suspended. To restore access, please verify your information immediately by clicking the link below:

http://secure-banking-verification.fraudulentsite.com

If you do not verify within 24 hours, your account will be permanently suspended.

Sincerely,
Security Department

Scam #2: Dating Profile Messages

Dating Profile: James Wilson

Age: 45 | Occupation: Military/Engineer working overseas

About me: Successful engineer currently on a military contract in [foreign country]. Widowed 3 years ago, looking for genuine connection. I believe in love at first sight and am ready to commit to the right person.

Messages after 2 weeks of chatting:

"I've never felt this connection before. I know we just met, but I think you're the one."

"I'm trying to come visit you next month, but I'm having trouble accessing my bank account overseas. Could you help me with a loan for the ticket? I'll pay you back as soon as I arrive."

Scam #3: Computer Pop-up Alert

⚠️ WARNING: VIRUS DETECTED ⚠️

Your computer has been infected with spyware. Your personal information and banking details are at risk!

Call Microsoft Support immediately: 1-800-123-4567

Do not close this window or restart your computer or all your data may be lost.

Phone Script:

"Hello, this is Jacob from Microsoft Technical Support. Our servers detected dangerous viruses on your computer. I can help you remove them, but we'll need remote access to your computer. We'll also require a security processing fee of $299 payable by credit card or gift cards..."

Fraud Protection Plan

Estimated time: 15 minutes

Now that you've analyzed various scams, it's time to develop your personal fraud protection plan based on your specific vulnerabilities.

Fraud Vulnerability Assessment

Answer these questions honestly to identify your personal risk areas.

1. How often do you check your financial accounts for suspicious activity?

2. Do you use the same password for multiple accounts?

3. How do you respond to unexpected emails requesting action?

4. Do you use two-factor authentication when available?

5. How often do you post personal information on social media?
