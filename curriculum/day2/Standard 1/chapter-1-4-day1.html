Chapter 1.4: Setting Financial Goals

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Understand the importance of setting clear financial goals
  • Apply the SMART criteria to create effective financial goals
  • Differentiate between short-term and long-term financial goals
  • Identify methods for prioritizing financial goals

Why Set Financial Goals?

Financial goals give you direction for your money decisions and help you track your progress. Without clear goals, it's easy to spend money without purpose, making it harder to achieve the things that matter most to you.

Think of financial goals as a roadmap for your money. Just as you would use a map or GPS to reach a destination, financial goals help guide your spending, saving, and investing decisions to reach your desired financial future.

Why Goals Matter

  • They provide direction and purpose for your financial decisions
  • They help you track progress and stay motivated
  • They transform vague wishes into actionable plans
  • They help you prioritize how you use your limited resources

Short-Term vs. Long-Term Goals

Short-Term Goals

Things you want to accomplish within the next year:

  • Saving for a new phone
  • Building an emergency fund
  • Saving for a concert ticket
  • Paying off a small debt
  • Purchasing a special gift

Long-Term Goals

Achievements that take more than a year to accomplish:

  • Saving for college
  • Buying a car
  • Funding a gap year or travel experience
  • Starting a business
  • Building retirement savings

Having a mix of both short-term and long-term goals creates a balanced financial plan. Short-term goals provide quick wins that keep you motivated, while long-term goals help you build toward significant life achievements.

The SMART Framework for Setting Goals

To create goals you can actually achieve, use the SMART criteria:

S Specific

Clearly define what you want to accomplish

M Measurable

Include a specific amount or metric to track progress

A Achievable

Be realistic about what you can accomplish given your resources

R Relevant

Make sure the goal aligns with your values and priorities

T Time-bound

Set a specific deadline for achieving your goal

Example: SMART Goal in Action

NOT SMART:

"I want to save money for a laptop."

This goal lacks specifics about how much money is needed, how it will be saved, and when it should be completed.

SMART Version:

"I will save $800 for a new laptop by saving $100 per month for 8 months, tracking my progress in a savings app."

Specific:
$800 laptop
Measurable:
$100/month
Achievable:
Realistic amount
Relevant:
Need for school/work
Time-bound:
8 months

Prioritizing Your Financial Goals

When you have multiple goals, prioritization helps you decide where to focus first:

1. Urgency

Which goals need to be accomplished sooner?

Consider deadlines, time-sensitive opportunities, and needs vs. wants

2. Importance

Which goals will have the biggest impact on your life?

Consider both short-term quality of life and long-term financial security

3. Feasibility

Which goals can you realistically achieve with your current resources?

Consider your income, existing savings, time constraints, and skills

Discussion Point

How would you prioritize these three financial goals? Explain your reasoning.

  • Saving $1,000 for an emergency fund (6 months)
  • Saving $300 for a new smartphone (3 months)
  • Saving $5,000 for a used car (2 years)

Case Studies

Riley's Approach

Riley wants to buy a $800 laptop for college in 8 months. Riley sets up automatic transfers of $100 each month to a savings account and tracks progress using a savings app.

Goal Implementation:

  • Created specific goal with exact amount
  • Set up automatic monthly transfers
  • Used tracking system to monitor progress
  • Set realistic timeline based on income
Result: Successfully reached the goal on time.

Morgan's Approach

Morgan wants to "save some money for the future." Without specific amounts or deadlines, Morgan doesn't track progress and spends inconsistently.

Goal Implementation:

  • Created vague goal with no specific amount
  • No regular saving schedule established
  • No tracking system implemented
  • No timeline or deadline set
Result: Has not made meaningful progress toward any financial goals.

The key difference between these approaches is that Riley used the SMART framework to create a clear, actionable plan with specific targets and tracking methods. Morgan's vague approach lacked the structure needed to make consistent progress.
