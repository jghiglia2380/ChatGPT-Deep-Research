Chapter 7.2: Choosing the Right Source of Credit

Learning Lab: Applying concepts through interactive activities

Learning Objectives

  • Apply critical thinking to evaluate appropriate credit sources for different situations
  • Calculate and compare the total cost of various credit options
  • Create a decision-making framework for responsible borrowing
  • Develop strategies to avoid high-cost predatory lending

Introduction

Welcome to the Learning Lab for Choosing the Right Source of Credit! Today, we'll build on what you learned about credit sources by applying that knowledge to real-world scenarios and developing a personal borrowing strategy. You'll analyze different borrowing situations, calculate the true cost of credit options, and create recommendations for various financial scenarios.

Important Note

The activities in this Learning Lab simulate real borrowing scenarios for educational purposes. In the real world, credit sources may have additional terms, conditions, and qualification requirements. Always research thoroughly and read the fine print before making any borrowing decisions.

Connection to Day 1

In Day 1, you learned about different sources of credit including banks, credit unions, online lenders, payday lenders, and more. You explored their typical interest rates, fees, qualification requirements, and appropriate use cases. Today's Learning Lab builds on that foundation by asking you to apply this knowledge to evaluate and choose the right credit sources for various situations.

Borrowing Decision Checklist:

  • Compare at least 3 offers before making a decision
  • Calculate the total cost (principal + interest + fees) for any loan

  • Check for prepayment penalties or hidden fees
  • Consider how this borrowing affects your debt-to-income ratio

Station 4: Predatory Lending Case Study

Estimated time: 15 minutes

In this activity, you'll examine real examples of predatory lending practices and develop strategies to identify warning signs, find alternative sources of credit, and access community resources for financial emergencies.

Predatory Lending Case Study

Case Study: The Payday Loan Cycle

Background: James works as a security guard earning $2,400 per month. After his car broke down, he needed $400 for repairs but had no emergency savings. His next paycheck was 10 days away, but he needed to get to work immediately.

The Situation: James visited a payday lender who offered him a $400 loan. The terms required him to repay $460 ($400 principal + $60 fee) in 14 days when his next paycheck arrived—an effective APR of 391%.

When the payment came due, James couldn't afford to both repay the loan and cover his regular expenses. The lender offered to "roll over" the loan for another 14 days for an additional $60 fee. James accepted.

After three rollovers and $240 in fees, James still owed the original $400. After six months, he had paid over $780 in fees alone but still owed the $400 principal.

Emergency Financial Resources

Below are resources that can help in financial emergencies instead of turning to predatory lenders:

Community Emergency Assistance

Many communities have emergency assistance programs for utilities, rent, and essential needs. Contact your local United Way by dialing 211 for referrals.

Credit Union Emergency Loans

Many credit unions offer small emergency loans with reasonable rates, even for members with fair credit.

Payment Plans

For medical bills, car repairs, and utilities, many providers will work out interest-free payment plans if you ask proactively.

Nonprofit Credit Counseling

Organizations like the National Foundation for Credit Counseling offer free advice and can help negotiate with creditors.
