Chapter 14.2: Checking Out Charitable Groups

Learning Lab: Analyzing and evaluating charitable organizations

<\!-- Learning Objectives Section -->

Learning Objectives

  • Apply professional tools and techniques to evaluate charitable organizations
  • Develop personal criteria for assessing charitable effectiveness based on your values
  • Create a strategic charitable giving plan that aligns with your priorities
  • Experience the decision-making process of a grant committee
  • Identify warning signs of fraudulent or problematic charitable organizations
<\!-- Introduction Section -->

Introduction

Welcome to the Learning Lab for Checking Out Charitable Groups\! Today, you'll apply what you learned about evaluating charitable organizations through hands-on activities. You'll analyze real nonprofits, develop evaluation criteria, create a personal giving plan, and participate in a mock grant committee. These interactive experiences will help you become a more informed and effective charitable contributor.

<\!-- Important Note -->

Important Note

This Learning Lab focuses on applying critical thinking to charitable giving decisions. While we'll analyze real organizations, remember that all evaluations are educational exercises and should not be considered formal endorsements or critiques of any particular organization.

<\!-- Connection to Day 1 -->

Connection to Day 1

In Day 1, you learned about various methods for evaluating charitable organizations, including financial ratios, transparency measures, and impact assessment. You also explored resources like Charity Navigator, GuideStar, and the IRS Form 990. Today's Learning Lab will give you practical experience using these tools and techniques to make informed giving decisions.

Community Impact Foundation Scenario

You are serving on a grant committee for the "Community Impact Foundation." Your committee has $10,000 to award to charitable organizations, with a minimum grant of $2,000 and a maximum of $5,000 per organization.

Grant Committee Guidelines

  • You must allocate the full $10,000 budget
  • Grants must be between $2,000 and $5,000 per organization
  • You may fund some, all, or none of each organization's requested amount
  • Your decisions should be based on merit, impact, and alignment with foundation priorities

Charity Scam Investigation

<\!-- Activity Timer -->
Estimated time: 15 minutes
<\!-- Station Content -->

In this activity, you'll learn to identify warning signs of fraudulent or problematic charitable organizations.

<\!-- Red Flag List -->

Common Red Flags in Charitable Organizations

  • Pressure for immediate donations
  • Thank you for previous donations you don't remember making
  • Similar names to well-known organizations
  • Vague mission statements or program descriptions
  • Limited or no financial information available
  • No proof of tax-exempt status
  • Excessive administrative or fundraising costs
  • No physical address or contact information

Charity Investigation Cases:

Examine the following profiles to identify potential red flags and concerning practices.

<\!-- Case 1 -->
Case 1: International Children's Relief Fund

This organization solicits donations for orphaned children in several developing countries. Their website features emotional images of children in need, with immediate donation buttons on every page. They claim that "90% of donations go directly to the children," but their website does not include any financial reports or specific program descriptions.

The organization has a similar name to a well-known international relief organization but is not affiliated with them. Contact information includes only an email address and a P.O. box.

Notable Features:

  • Established: Claims to be operating since 2010
  • Website: Professional looking with emotional appeals
  • Donations: Credit card, wire transfer, or cryptocurrency
  • Transparency: No annual reports or financial statements
  • Solicitation: Email campaigns with urgent appeals
  • Media presence: No media coverage or third-party reviews
<\!-- Case 2 -->
Case 2: Hometown Veterans Support Alliance

This local organization raises funds for veterans' services in your community. They operate through phone solicitations and community events. Their representatives mention specific local veterans by name and have detailed knowledge of the community. They have a small but functional website that includes their physical address, staff names, and board members (some of whom are recognizable community figures).

They acknowledge that 30% of donations go to fundraising and administrative costs, with the remainder supporting various veterans' programs. They're a newer organization, established 3 years ago.

Notable Features:

  • Established: Operating for 3 years
  • Website: Basic site with program descriptions
  • Donations: Credit card, check, or in-person
  • Transparency: Shows fundraising costs, no detailed financial reports
  • Solicitation: Phone calls and local events
  • Media presence: Some local news coverage of their events
<\!-- Case 3 -->
Case 3: Save Our Parks Foundation

This environmental organization focuses on preserving local parks and green spaces. Their professional website includes their mission, specific programs, staff bios, and board members. They have a physical office and provide multiple contact methods. Their financial information shows 75% of funds going to programs, 15% to administration, and 10% to fundraising.

They publish an annual report detailing their activities and impact, including the number of acres preserved and educational programs conducted. The organization is registered as a 501(c)(3) with the IRS, and this information is prominently displayed on their website.

Notable Features:

  • Established: Operating for 12 years
  • Website: Comprehensive with detailed program information
  • Donations: Multiple options including monthly giving
  • Transparency: Publishes annual reports and audited financials
  • Solicitation: Email, website, community events
  • Media presence: Regular coverage in local media
<\!-- Case 4 -->
Case 4: Disaster Recovery Network

This organization appears immediately after natural disasters, soliciting urgent donations for relief efforts. Their website includes dramatic images of disaster scenes and emotional appeals. They claim to work across multiple countries and disaster types, but provide few specifics about their programs or how funds are used.

The organization claims to be tax-exempt but does not provide an EIN (Employer Identification Number) for verification. The "About Us" page is vague about the organization's history and leadership. Their contact page lists only an email address and an online form.

Notable Features:

  • Established: Claims to be established but no date provided
  • Website: Professional design but limited content
  • Donations: Credit card only, with suggested minimum of $50
  • Transparency: No financial information or reports
  • Solicitation: Social media ads targeting disaster news
  • Media presence: None verifiable

Discussion Question:

What strategies can consumers use to protect themselves from charity scams while still supporting worthy causes?
