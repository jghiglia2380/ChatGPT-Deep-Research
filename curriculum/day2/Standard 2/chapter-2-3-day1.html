Chapter 2.3: Understanding Tax Filing Requirements

Filing taxes is an important part of adult financial life. In this lesson, you'll learn the basics of tax filing requirements - who needs to file taxes, what forms and documents are needed, and key concepts like filing status and dependents. By the end, you'll have a foundation to fulfill your tax obligations.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Understand who is required to file federal income taxes
  • Learn about the different IRS tax forms and when to use them
  • Determine your tax filing status
  • Identify who qualifies as a dependent

Who Needs to File Taxes?

In general, U.S. citizens and residents must file a federal income tax return if their annual gross income exceeds certain thresholds. For tax year 2022:

  • Single filers under age 65: gross income of $12,950 or more
  • Single filers 65 or older: gross income of $14,700 or more
  • Married filing jointly under 65 (both spouses): gross income of $25,900 or more
  • Married filing jointly 65 or older (one spouse): gross income of $27,300 or more
  • Married filing jointly 65 or older (both spouses): gross income of $28,700 or more

There are some exceptions, such as self-employment income over $400 which requires filing a return regardless of total income.

Common Tax Forms

The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) uses various forms to collect information about your income and determine your tax liability. Here are the most common forms:

Form W-2: Wage and Tax Statement

Your employer provides this form showing wages earned and taxes withheld for the year. You'll receive this by January 31st for the previous tax year.

Form 1040: U.S. Individual Income Tax Return

The standard form most taxpayers use to file their annual federal income tax return. There are several variations:

  • Form 1040-SR: For taxpayers age 65 or older
  • Form 1040-NR: For nonresident aliens
  • Form 1040-X: Used to amend a previously filed return

Form 1098: Mortgage Interest Statement

Shows mortgage interest paid during the year, which may be deductible.

Form 1099: Miscellaneous Income

Several variations exist to report different types of income:

  • 1099-INT: Interest income
  • 1099-DIV: Dividend income
  • 1099-MISC: Miscellaneous income
  • 1099-NEC: Nonemployee compensation (such as freelance work)

Understanding Filing Status

Your filing status determines your tax rate, standard deduction amount, and eligibility for certain credits. There are five filing statuses:

  • Single: You're unmarried, divorced, or legally separated.
  • Married Filing Jointly: You're married and both spouses agree to file together.
  • Married Filing Separately: You're married but choose to file separate returns.
  • Head of Household: You're unmarried, paid more than half the cost of keeping up a home, and have a qualifying person living with you.
  • Qualifying Widow(er): Your spouse died in the previous two years, you haven't remarried, and you have a dependent child.

Case Study: Determining Filing Status

Jasmine is 26 years old and recently divorced. She has a 6-year-old daughter who lives with her full-time. Jasmine pays all housing expenses and provides more than half of her daughter's financial support.

What would Jasmine's most advantageous filing status be?

Answer: Head of Household. This status would give her a higher standard deduction than filing as Single and could make her eligible for additional tax credits like the Earned Income Credit.

Determining Dependents

Claiming dependents can provide tax benefits. A dependent is typically a qualifying child or qualifying relative.

Qualifying Child

Must meet these tests:

  • Relationship: Your child, stepchild, foster child, sibling, or descendant
  • Age: Under 19, or under 24 if a full-time student
  • Residency: Must live with you for more than half the year
  • Support: Must not provide more than half of their own support

Qualifying Relative

Must meet these tests:

  • Not a qualifying child of any taxpayer
  • Related to you, or lived with you all year as a member of your household
  • Gross income less than $4,400 (for 2022)
  • You provided more than half of their total support for the year

Skill Builder: Tax Filing Requirements Analysis

Tax Filing Requirements Analysis

In this activity, you'll analyze different scenarios to determine whether individuals need to file taxes, what forms they need, and what filing status they should use.

Scenario 1: Alex

Alex is 17 years old and made $3,500 last year working part-time at a local restaurant. His parents claim him as a dependent on their tax return.

Alex doesn't need to file because his income is below the threshold for dependents ($12,950 in 2022). However, he might want to file to get a refund of any withheld taxes.

Scenario 2: Maria

Maria is 35, unmarried, and has a 7-year-old daughter who lives with her. Maria earned $45,000 last year as a nurse. She pays all housing costs and provides most of her daughter's financial support.

Maria should file as Head of Household and can claim her daughter as a dependent. This filing status offers more favorable tax rates and a higher standard deduction than filing as Single.

Scenario 3: James and David

James and David are married. James earned $75,000 as a software developer. David earned $15,000 from a part-time job and has $30,000 in student loan debt.

James and David would typically benefit from filing jointly. This gives them access to certain tax credits and a higher standard deduction. However, in some cases with student loan debt, filing separately might be beneficial if David is on an income-based repayment plan.
