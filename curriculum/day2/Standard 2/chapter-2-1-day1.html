Introduction

In this lesson, you'll explore how federal and state taxes impact your income and how to plan your budget accordingly. You'll learn about different types of taxes, how they're calculated, and how they fund essential public services.

Where Does the Money Come From?

Think about the services you use daily—schools, roads, healthcare, police—how do you think these are funded? Where does the money come from?

Key Terms

Federal Income Tax

Tax collected by the federal government based on personal or business income.

Progressive Tax

A tax system where the tax rate increases as the taxable amount increases.

Payroll Taxes

Taxes withheld from employee paychecks for Social Security and Medicare.

Social Security Tax

Tax on earnings that funds the Social Security program.

Medicare Tax

Tax on earnings that helps fund Medicare health insurance.

State Income Tax

Tax collected by individual states on income, varies by state.

Sales Tax

Tax charged on goods and services at the point of purchase.

Property Tax

Tax assessed on real estate and certain personal property.

Tax Bracket

Income range subject to a specific tax rate in a progressive tax system.

Withholding

Amount of tax deducted from your paycheck and sent to the government.

Exploring Federal Income Tax

Federal income tax is based on a progressive tax system, meaning tax rates increase as your income increases. Let's examine how federal tax brackets work and what that means for your take-home pay.

How Progressive Taxes Work

In a progressive tax system, different portions of your income are taxed at increasing rates as your income rises. This means that:

  • Your first dollars earned are taxed at the lowest rate
  • As you earn more, additional dollars are taxed at higher rates
  • Only the portion of income that falls within a specific bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate
  • You don't pay the higher rate on all your income, just the portion in that bracket

This system is designed to ensure that those with higher incomes contribute proportionally more in taxes, while protecting lower-income earners.

State Income Tax Comparison

Each state has its own tax system. Some states like Texas and Florida have no state income tax, while others like California have higher income tax rates.

Comparing State Tax Approaches

Would you prefer to live in a state with no income tax but higher property and sales taxes, or a state with income tax but potentially lower other taxes? What factors would influence your decision?

State Tax Approaches

Approach Example States Key Features
No State Income Tax Florida, Texas, Nevada, Washington Higher sales taxes, property taxes, or other fees to compensate
Flat Tax Rate Illinois, Colorado, Michigan Same percentage regardless of income level
Progressive Tax California, New York, Oregon Higher rates as income increases, similar to federal system

Payroll Taxes and Your Paycheck

Beyond income taxes, every employee pays payroll taxes (FICA) to fund Social Security and Medicare. These taxes are automatically deducted from your paycheck.

Sample Pay Stub Analysis

Paycheck for: Jane Smith

Pay Period: 1/1/2025 - 1/15/2025

Earnings

Gross Pay: $3,000.00

Deductions

Federal Income Tax: $450.00
State Income Tax: $150.00
Social Security (6.2%): $186.00
Medicare (1.45%): $43.50
Health Insurance: $120.00
Total Deductions: $949.50
Net Pay: $2,050.50

Notice how different taxes impact the take-home pay:

  • Federal income tax: based on tax brackets and withholding choices
  • State income tax: varies by state
  • Social Security: fixed rate of 6.2% on earnings up to annual limit
  • Medicare: fixed rate of 1.45% on all earnings

Case Study Analysis

Tax Impact Across States

Mia in California

Annual Salary: $75,000

State Income Tax: ~$4,200 (progressive system)

Property Taxes: Lower rate but higher property values

Sales Tax: 7.25% state + local taxes

Advantages: More state-funded services, potentially lower property taxes

Disadvantages: Higher overall tax burden, complicated tax filing

Liam in Texas

Annual Salary: $75,000

State Income Tax: $0 (no state income tax)

Property Taxes: Higher rates (~2-3% of home value)

Sales Tax: 6.25% state + up to 2% local

Advantages: No state income tax, potentially lower overall tax burden

Disadvantages: Higher property taxes, less state funding for services

Both Mia and Liam have the same gross income, but their tax situations and take-home pay differ significantly due to state tax structures. Consider how this might impact:

  • Housing affordability in each location
  • Quality and availability of public services
  • Overall cost of living and financial planning

Skill Builder Activity: Tax System Analysis

Tax System Analysis

For each scenario below, decide whether a progressive tax system or a regressive tax system would be more appropriate. Consider each person's ability to pay, the impact on their financial situation, and the potential benefits received.

Progressive Tax System:

A system where higher-income individuals pay a higher percentage of their income in taxes.

Regressive Tax System:

A system where lower-income individuals pay a higher percentage of their income in taxes compared to higher-income individuals.

Scenario 1: John – Entry-Level Worker

  • Income: $25,000 per year
  • Job: Works full-time as a retail associate
  • Financial Situation: Has no dependents, but struggles to afford basic living expenses such as rent, utilities, and groceries
  • Other Information: Limited access to government services and no discretionary income for savings or investments

Scenario 2: Samantha – Mid-Career Professional

  • Income: $80,000 per year
  • Job: Works as a marketing manager for a mid-sized company
  • Financial Situation: Supports a family of four, owns a home, and has substantial mortgage and childcare expenses
  • Other Information: Uses a mix of public and private services (e.g., public schooling for children, local public transit) and has a small investment portfolio

Scenario 3: Andrew – High-Earning Business Owner

  • Income: $500,000 per year
  • Job: Owns a successful real estate investment business
  • Financial Situation: Has multiple income streams and significant assets, including several properties. Uses various tax loopholes and deductions to minimize tax liabilities
  • Other Information: Has access to private services (e.g., private schooling for children) and receives less direct benefit from government-funded programs
